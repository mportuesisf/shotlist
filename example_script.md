### 00 Why hand stitch?

00  
Most sewists will do anything to avoid hand-sewing, because a sewing machine is so much faster.  But it’s a really handy skill that broadens the kinds of projects you can tackle.

01  
Hand stitches can get into places a sewing machine cannot, and for certain tasks, a hand stitch is the only way to get the job done.

02  
Other sewing tasks, such as hemming, can be done by hand or on the machine, but you usually get a better result if you do it by hand.  That’s because hand sewing gives you a greater level of control than machine stitching.

### 10 Needles

10  
Hand sewing needles are different from sewing machine needles. There are many types, but in this video we’ll talk only about the two most common types that you will use for most sewing tasks.

11  
Sharps are your basic, all-purpose sewing needle that you will use on most projects.  They are good at making longer stitches.

12  
Betweens (also called quilting needles) are smaller than sharps in general, and are good for making shorter stitches, or for going through several layers of fabric.  They are also useful for fine sewing work such as applying lace, or for attaching applique, beads, and sequins.

13  
Sharps and betweens are rated in size from 1 to 12. Contrary to sewing machine needles, the larger the number, the shorter and finer the needle. Generally, sizes 7 to 10 are what you need for most hand sewing tasks.  A sharp is larger than a between with the same size rating.

14  
Generally, you want to choose the smallest needle that will work nicely with your fabric.  The lighter the fabric weight, the smaller the needle size.

15  
Lighter and thinner needles will go through fabric easier.  You won’t need to apply so much force with your fingers, which in turn allows you to make a more accurate stitch.

16  
But you may need a heavier, thicker needle in order to pass through several layers of fabric, or with thick fabrics such as denim, or canvas.

17  
The type of stitch also influences your choice of needle. For basting and other types of temporary stitching that involve long stitches, use a longer needle.  For finer stitches, use a shorter needle.

18  
Ultimately, the choice of needle is a personal preference.  Choose what is most comfortable for you.

### 20 Thread

20  
You can use general-purpose sewing machine thread, but thread made for hand-sewing is easier to handle, and is less likely to knot or tangle while sewing. Hand-sewing thread is often cotton or silk, rather than the polyester of most general-purpose machine threads.

21  
Whatever you do, choose quality thread. Discount threads from the fabric store often have rough strands that will quickly snarl and tangle if used for hand-sewing.

22  
Make sure the thread is appropriate for the fabric. Generally, you want to match thread type to fabric type. Choose a cotton thread for cottons and other natural fibers such as linen, and a polyester thread for synthetic fabrics.

23  
Choose a silk thread for working with silk and other silky fabrics, wool, or for situations when the hand stitching will be highly visible, as in a buttonhole. Genuine silk thread is excellent for hand-sewing, because it is smooth and less likely to tangle.

24  
There is also a “silk finish” cotton thread, that is cotton thread but treated to make it more shiny and smooth, like silk.

25  
Finally, make sure the thread is the appropriate weight for the needle and fabric.  The heavier the fabric, the larger the needle and the heavier the thread you must use.  If the thread is too heavy, it will be hard to thread the needle.  If the thread is too lightweight, it can snarl or knot, or create a weak seam.

### 30 Beeswax and thread conditioner

30  
Another way to prevent knots and tangles is to treat the thread using beeswax, or a synthetic thread conditioner.  These products lubricate the thread and allow it to more easily pass through fabric.

31  
To wax the thread, place each strand on the wax, hold it in place with your thumb, then pull it through along the length of the strand.

32  
After waxing, you can press the thread with a not-too-hot iron, between layers of paper towel. This allows the wax to set between the fibers of the thread, takes out any twists, and removes any excess wax that may leave residue on your project.

33  
If you are using a synthetic thread conditioner, there is no need to press the thread after treating it.  Thread conditioners can darken or discolor thread, so check before you sew.

### 40 Thimbles

40  
A thimble protects your finger as you push the needle through fabric. It allows you to handle the needle and fabric more securely, giving you greater control and precision over your handstitches.

41  
Thimbles are made with many different types of materials. Metal thimbles are stronger and more suitable for working with heavy materials. Leather or rubber thimblesare more flexible and can feel more comfortable.

42  
Plastic thimbles are kind of an in-between - they are more durable than leather, but they are softer, more comfortable, and less rigid than metal.

43  
Thimbles also have two basic designs. The Tailor’s Thimble is open-ended. It can give you more dexterity and control, because the end of your finger is open to feel the fabric. The opening also provides ventilation, so it can be worn for longer periods of time.

44  
The Dressmaker’s Thimble, by contrast, is closed-ended. It provides more protection for your hands.

45  
The thimble goes on your middle finger, and it must fit your hand properly. A tailor’s thimble should allow your finger to protrude slightly from the open end.

46  
Try to hang your hand downward and move your fingers around. The thimble should stay on, without sliding around.

47  
Once you have found a thimble that stays on, try using it to see if it is comfortable and feels natural to use. You may need to try several styles before you find the one that’s best for you.

### 50 Other tools

50  
The Needle Grabber is a small, flat piece of rubber or other material with a lot of grip.  It allows you to get a better grip on your needle, to pull it through the fabric. It’s an alternative to a thimble that you might prefer.

51  
A Needle Threader makes it easier to thread needles, especially smaller needles with very fine eyes.  If you have poor eyesight, this can be a lifesaver.

### 60 Threading the needle

60  
Thread the needle with a single strand.  Don’t cut your thread strand too long, or it will tangle and knot while stitching. Keep it under 18 inches (½ meter), or the length of your forearm.

61  
It’s easier to thread the needle when you cut the thread end cleanly. It also helps to cut at an angle.

62  
Hold the end of the thread between your thumb and forefinger. Then, place the eyelet of the needle to the end of the thread. This is easier than trying to push the thread through the eye of the needle.

63  
To thread using a needle threader, first push the wire loop through the eye of the needle. Then, thread the wire loop. Finally, pull the wire loop back out of the needle.

64  
Thread several needles at once, before you start sewing, to keep your sewing flow going.

### 70 Fabric handling and posture

70  
You can sew in your lap while watching TV, but you are likely to get the best results stitching with your fabric supported flat, while seated over a table.  You’ll get more even stitches, and you’ll avoid over-handling the fabric.

71  
A pressing ham makes a great support for both your project, as well as your hands while stitching.

72  
I also like a magnifier for situations where I need to sew with better precision. This lamp has a magnifier built in, or you can get magnifying glasses.

73  
If you are right-handed, you will stitch from right-to-left. There are some exceptions, such as the catchstitch, which we’ll cover later.  If you are left-handed, you’ll do the reverse, stitching most stitches left-to-right.

### 80 Starting and ending the stitch

80  
You must anchor any stitch that will be a permanent part of your project - at the start and end, or else it will unravel. There are two main methods to do this.

81  
The first is to make a knot at the far end of the strand. Hold the threaded needle in one hand, between the thumb and forefinger.

82  
Wrap the other end around the needle, about five or six times.  Leave about an inch of thread tail.

83  
Grasp the needle with your other hand and pull it through. Keep pulling all the way along the length of thread. A knot will form at the end of the thread, between your thumb and forefinger.

84  
Begin the stitch by inserting the needle from in-between the fabric layers, or from the wrong side, so the knot will be hidden inside the seam. You can also take a small backstitch here to be certain the knot is secure.

85  
The other method to anchor the start is to take some tiny backstitches.  Place two or three small backstitches atop each other to anchor the thread. Avoiding the knot also avoids bulk in the seam, which could show as a bump with thin or delicate fabrics.

86  
At the end of the stitch, you can tie it off with a loop. Take a small pick right at the end of the last stitch, then pull until the loop is small but still open. Run the needle through the small loop, creating a new loop.

87  
Then, run the needle back around and through the new, larger loop you just created, creating a figure-eight. Finally, pull the needle tight to form a secure knot.

### 90 Using thimbles and needle grabbers

90  
The thimble is helpful when working with heavier fabrics, or with many layers. Place the thimble on your middle finger.  Hold the needle between your thumb and forefinger, with the needle perpendicular to the index finger.  The thimble sits right up against the eyelet end of the needle.

91  
Take a pick of the fabric with the needle.  Then with your middle finger, use the thimble to push the needle through the stitch. Your thumb and index finger guides the needle as your middle finger pushes it through.

92  
Alternatively, you can use a needle grabber to grasp and pull the needle through the stitch. Take a pick, place the grabber between thumb and forefinger, grab the needle, and pull it through.

### 100 Safety and needle care

100  
Before we move on, one last note. If you need to take a break or change tasks, make sure you have a place to store the needle. Untended needles find their way to the floor, where people and pets can step on them.

101  
A pincushion or magnetic pin holder works well to hold needles when you are not stitching.

### 110 The Running Stitch

110  
The Running Stitch is a single row of stitching with gaps in-between each stitch. You can use it for sewing seams, where the strength of the seam is not a big concern. It’s commonly used for basting, which you’ll see in a moment.

111  
To begin, start the stitch as we covered earlier in the video. Take a pick of the fabric with the needle. The needle goes through both layers, to the underside, and back again through both layers to the topside.

112  
Then pull the needle through. Once the needle is clear, pull the thread taut, but not tight.  One stitch is now complete.

113  
Viewed from one side, the stitches will have spaces between them. Keep doing this to make the stitch, and adjust layers as you go.

114  
When you reach the end of the strand of thread, or the end of the seam, end the stitch as we covered earlier in the video.

115  
To stitch faster, you can take several picks at once.  Take a pick of the needle as before, except insert the needle in and out to take several picks with one pull of the needle. A longer needle makes this easier.

116  
You may be limited with the number of stitches you can take at once, if you are using several layers or heavy fabrics.

### 120 The Basting Stitch

120  
The Basting Stitch is a running stitch made with really long stitches.  You can use it to temporarily hold difficult or slippery fabrics in place before stitching the final seam, usually on a sewing machine.

121  
You can also use it during fitting and alterations to make new temporary seamlines that you finish on the sewing machine.

122  
The basting stitch is a temporary stitch, so there’s no need to worry about matching thread colors. Choose a contrasting thread for easy visibility.

123  
At the beginning and end, there is no need to securely anchor the stitch.  Instead, just use a single backstitch to keep the basting in place for long enough to stitch the final seam.

124  
Compared to a regular running stitch, each pick of the needle is ⅜ to ½ inch wide, and needle picks are farther apart.  There is no need to be neat: these are temporary stitches, and not meant to be in the final project.

125  
Take several picks at once with the needle to work quickly. You can get special basting needles that are extra-long and meant to take several picks with one pull.

126  
Baste right along the stitching line, especially if you are basting to test out fit.  Or baste right inside the seam allowance, just against the line of stitching.

127  
After you have sewed the permanent seam, pull the basting with a tool that will not snag or tear the fabric. You can get a special basting thread which is glazed.  It is easy to see, and pulls out of the fabric easily.

### 130 The Backstitch

130  
The backstitch simulates a line of machine stitching.  It is strong enough for seams in clothing, and before  there were sewing machines, this is how most clothing was made. Hand-tailored and couture garments are still made today entirely with handstitching.

131  
Besides seaming for garment construction, you can also use it to put in zippers, for hand quilting, or for decorative topstitching.

132  
To begin, start the stitch as we covered earlier in the video.  Bring the needle backwards from the entry point by one stitch length, and take a pick.

133  
Run the needle under the fabric, and past the entry point by one stitch length. Then bring the needle back to the upward side of the fabric, and pull the needle through. The stitch is started.

134  
To make each stitch, take a pick right at the end of the previous stitch. Run the needle underneath and one stitch length past the new stitch, then come back up.

135  
Once the needle is through, pull the thread taut, but not tight. Keep doing this to make the stitch, adjusting layers as you go.

136  
When you reach the end of the strand of thread, or the end of the seam, end the stitch as we covered earlier in the video.

### 140 The Pickstitch

140  
The pickstitch is a variant of the backstitch. The upper side of the stitch has much smaller stitches than the underside.

141  
The pickstitch is ideal for backstitched seams through thick fabrics, or many layers of fabric that would not feed well through a sewing machine, or be damaged by it such as velvet.  However, it is less strong than an even backstitch.

142  
The pickstitch is also used in fine sewing for installing zippers, and understitching facings: It uses only a single thread, compared to two threads used by a machine stitch.  The result is less bulk and a softer finish.

143  
Finally, the pickstitch can be used for a decorative topstitching effect.

144  
You make the pickstitch the exact same way as the topstitch, except that the top pick is short. The needle goes underneath much further out than one stitch length, before it resurfaces to the topside.

145  
Remember to pull the thread taut, but not tight, to close the stitch. You do want the “picks” of the stitch to show along the top side.

### 150 The Slipstitch

150  
The Slipstitch goes by several names.  It’s also known as the invisible stitch, ladder stitch, or mattress stitch. It is almost invisible when finished, because the stitches lie inside the seam.

151  
Unlike the other stitches we’ve covered, which work with two layers of fabric right sides together, with the slipstitch you work from the right side. It can join a folded edge to a flat layer, or two folded edges to each other.

152  
You can use it to invisibly attach one fabric to another, for example to close open areas along seamlines (for example to close a pillow, or a jacket lining).

153  
You can also use it to apply trimming, bindings, and appliques.  It is a very versatile stitch and is useful in many situations where using a sewing machine is infeasible.

154  
It is also an easy stitch to sew.  Because it’s invisible, if you have challenges sewing evenly it will still produce a nice-looking result.

155  
To begin, start the stitch as we covered earlier in the video. At the point the thread comes out, make a pick at the exact same spot on the other side.

156  
Tunnel along for one stitch length, then bring the pick back up, and pick the other side at the same point, following the stitch line. To keep the stitches even and hidden, tunnel the stitch slightly inside the fold or stitch line.

157  
To stitch faster, you can take more than one stitch with a single pull of the needle, just like we did earlier with the running stitch.

158  
Repeat this three or four times, then pull the thread taut, not tight, to close the seam. If you pull too tight, you’ll get a ripple.

159  
When you reach the end of the strand of thread, or the end of the seam, end the stitch as covered earlier in the video.

### 160 The Catchstitch

160  
The catchstitch looks like a row of “X”-es. It’s very useful for hemming, because it looks invisible from the outside. It has some give, which makes it suitable for stretch fabrics.

161  
Unlike the other stitches we’ve covered, you’ll work the catchstitch from left-to-right. (If you are left-handed, that’s from right-to-left).

162  
To begin, start the stitch as we covered earlier in the video.  Advance about ¼ inch to the right.  On the wrong side, grab just a thread or two of the fabric. The stitch should not be visible from the outside.

163  
Pull the stitch through, then cross over, advance another ¼ inch, and go back to the other side.

164  
Don’t pull too tightly.  The stitch should be taut, but it also must have some give. Also, pulling too tight will cause puckers.

165  
When you reach the end of the strand of thread, or the end of the seam, end the stitch as we covered earlier in the video.

### 170 Troubleshooting

170  
Let’s look at some of the difficulties you may encounter, and how to solve them.

**171**  
**If the needle comes unthreaded** while stitching, just rethread and continue stitching.

**172**  
**Most seams are longer than one strand of thread**, so you will frequently run out of thread while stitching. Try to end the stitch while you still have enough thread left to properly secure the end of the stitch.

173  
Start a new stitch where you left off, and continue the seam.  When you rethread, avoid the temptation to cut the thread long to try to reduce the number of times you must restart the stitch.  You’ll just get thread tangles and knots.

**174**  
**If the thread knots/tangles while stitching**, there are some things you can do to try to save it.  Sometimes you can untangle the knot by pulling the thread gently on both sides.

175  
Otherwise, you must clip off the knot, end the stitch, and start a new one. Cut as close to the knot as you can, so you will hopefully have enough thread to tie off and end the stitch.

176  
Twisted threads are a major cause of knots, and you can untwist the thread in advance to prevent them. Start with the end of the thread closest to your project. Grasp the thread between your thumb and forefinger, then pull your thumb and forefinger along the thread.

177  
You can avoid knots and tangles by using a better quality thread, using a thread meant for hand sewing, and by treating the thread with beeswax or a thread conditioner.

**178**  
**If the stitch looks puckered**, you’ve applied too much tension to the stitch. If you haven’t yet ended the stitch, you can often gently loosen and reset the stitch so the seam looks clean.

**179**  
**If the stitch looks loose**, you haven’t applied enough tension to the stitch.  The result will be a weak seam, with stitches showing. Again, if you haven’t yet ended the stitch, you can often gently tighten the stitches so the seam looks clean.

**180**  
**If the stitch looks uneven**, undo the stitch and try again. Try to make your stitches a consistent length, keep thread tension consistent, and sew in a straight line.

181  
To sew in a straight line, make a guide line. You can use chalk or washable marker, or you can apply a line of removable tape, but test the tape first to make sure it doesn’t damage the fabric.  Or simply make a crease in the fabric and sew along that.

### 190 Improving your hand stitching

190  
The quality of your stitches will improve as you master control of the needle and fabric. Try to hold the needle just below the eye. This will make controlling the needle and taking picks much easier.

191  
Consistency is key, in terms of length of the needle picks, straightness of the line of stitching, and thread tension. Consistent stitching not only looks best, and is also the strongest seam for your project.

192  
Finally, practice makes perfect. Do some simple hand-sewing projects.  As you complete them, your speed and consistency will improve.
